At a lower ks, we can see that the cloth is wrapping the sphere a lot more tightly than when ks is high. This is related to the effect that we saw in Part 2; a lower spring constant allows the the cloth to be free and thus more "jiggly". Similarly, in this case, a lower spring constant allows the cloth to wrap around the sphere more tightly. On the other hand, a higher spring constant restricts the movement of the cloth, which is why the cloth doesn't wrap itself around the sphere because the higher spring constant restricts the cloth.

When ks is lower, the cloth seems to be more crumpled up at rest state than when the ks is higher. When ks = 1, we can see that the cloth is really crumpled up at its rest state. This is probably due to the fact that a higher ks means a higher spring constant, so the springs are stiffer, meaning a higher ks would mean the springs have less displacement. When ks is lower, the springs have a higher displacement, meaning the cloth is prone to crumple more in its rest state.

Part 5

Shader program runs on the GPU and generates the final image with lighting and material effects. Vertex shaders are responsible for processing the geometry of 3D models. They take in information about the position, orientation, and scale of each vertex of a 3D model, and they use this information to transform the vertices into their final positions on the screen. On the other hand, fragment shaders are responsible for processing the fragments calculated by the vertex shader. They take in information about the position, color, and texture of each pixel, and they use this information to calculate the final color of the pixel.

The Blinn-Phong model uses a combination of ambient, diffuse, and specular lighting to create a more realistic lighting effect on the object. Ambient light is the general illumination that is present everywhere in the scene. Diffuse light is the light that is scattered in all directions when it hits a rough surface. Specular light is the light that is reflected in a specific direction when it hits a shiny surface. Each of these are calculated using the formulas given in lecture with surface normal, view direction, and light direction.
